1. What Constitutes Bishul

The Issur Doraisa: What Gets Cooked? The Melacha of bishul (Av Melacha: cooking/baking - 'haofeh') covers any significant transformation of food or material through heat.

Yad Soledes Bo (YSB) - The Temperature Threshold Bishul only takes effect at or above the temperature of Yad soledes bo. Below this temperature there is no Torah prohibition. One may not rely on one's own hand to test this - the assessment is subjective and may differ from Talmudic times.

AuthorityPositionNotes / Key Point
Rav Moshe FeinsteinMust be concerned from 43°C / ~109°FMost stringent
Rav Bentsion Abba ShaulMust be concerned from 40°C / ~104°FMost stringent
Rav Shlomo Zalman AuerbachYSB begins at 45°C / ~113°F (based on goose blood measurement)Most lenient of the three

Practical Rule: Treat anything above 40°C (104°F) as potentially YSB. You cannot test with your hand. Common benchmark: food that you cannot hold in your closed hand without discomfort is at or above YSB.

Maachal Ben Drussai (MB'D) - The 'Begun Cooking' Threshold A person is chayav for bishul once the food reaches the equivalent of one-third cooked (Rashi) or one-half cooked (Rambam) - the point at which a person would eat the food only in a time of great need (b'shaas hadchak).

2. Ein Bishul Achar Bishul - No Recooking of Cooked Food

The Core Rule Once food has been fully cooked, there is no issur doraisa to cook it again in the same medium. This is the principle of 'Ein bishul achar bishul' (EBA'B).

5. Kli Rishon, Kli Sheini, and Kli Shlishi

Definitions and Status Kli Rishon (KR): The vessel in which food was cooked on the fire. Its walls retain heat and can continue to cook food even off the fire - its cooking status is DORAISA even off the fire. Irui Kli Rishon: Pouring from a KR onto food. Cooks a klipa (outer layer) of dry food - treated as Kli sheini for most purposes. Into liquid: see below. Kli Sheini (KS): A vessel into which hot food/liquid has been poured from a KR. Its walls have cooled the liquid - does not cook midoraisa (in most cases). Raw food may be placed in it (with exceptions - see below). Kli Shlishi (KSH): A vessel into which food has been poured from a KS. Generally no concern at all. Rav Auerbach: kalei habishul forbidden even in a KSH (midoraisa). Rav Moshe / Chazon Ish / Rav Berkovits: permitted.

Kli Rishon - Off the Fire: Does It Still Cook?

Halacha: A Kli Rishon that is off the fire can still cook midoraisa as long as it is above YSB. Therefore: do not add raw uncooked ingredients to a Kli Rishon even off the fire if it is YSB.

Quick Summary: Core Principles

The 10 Most Important Rules of Hilchos Bishul:

  1. BISHUL APPLIES TO ALL FOOD: Assur midoraisa to cook any food - even if edible raw - if cooking improves it.
  2. YSB THRESHOLD: Treat anything above 40°C as potentially YSB. Cannot test with your hand.
  3. EIN BISHUL ACHAR BISHUL - DRY FOOD: Once dry food is fully cooked, may reheat freely (no EBA'B).
  4. EIN BISHUL ACHAR BISHUL - LIQUID: Once liquid cools to below eating temperature, forbidden to reheat (Ashkenaz: Shulchan Aruch HaRav / Rav Moshe / Rav Berkovits). Remove from heat if it dropped below this point.
  5. BISHUL ACHAR TSLI: Ashkenazim: do not cook previously baked/roasted food. Kli Sheini or Kli Rishon - forbidden for bread. Solution: use Kli Shlishi.
  6. KLI RISHON OFF FIRE: Still cooks midoraisa if YSB. Do not add raw food.
  7. KLI SHEINI: Mutar to put water, oil, salt, whole spices. Forbidden: bread, eggs, tea (kalei habishul), ground spices (Rav Auerbach / Rav Berkovits), most other raw foods (Magen Avraham / Mishnah Berurah).
  8. KLI SHLISHI: Generally permitted for everything. Exceptions: teabag (Rav Auerbach), but Rav Moshe / Rav Berkovits lenient.
  9. DVAR GUSH: Treat hot pieces of food as Kli Rishon (Mishnah Berurah / Ashkenaz stringency). Exception: surrounded by gravy (half covered) = Kli Sheini. Combine with ladle = Kli Shlishi in plate.
  10. LADLE: If pot was off fire - ladle is always Kli Sheini; plate = Kli Shlishi (Chazon Ish / Rav Berkovits). This makes most Shabbos table scenarios more manageable.

Hilchos Shabbos Hatmana, Shehiya, and Chazara

1. Hatmana - Insulating a Pot Hatmana is the Rabbinic prohibition of surrounding a pot to retain or add heat. There are two categories: (a) Dvar hamosif hevel - a covering that actively adds its own heat; (b) Dvar she'eino mosif hevel - a covering that merely retains heat.

  2. Asking a non-Jew to do an issur derabanan is permitted for a mitzva, great need, or miksas choli (Rambam / Mechaber - SA 307:5).
  3. Asking for a melacha doraisa for a mitzva: generally strict. Exceptions: fixing an Eiruv (Mishnah Berurah); mitzva d'rabbim (Rav Moshe).
  4. Benefiting from non-Jew's melacha: positive benefit forbidden; removing a nuisance (negative benefit) permitted.
  5. Adding light to an already-lit room: permitted. Turning on light in a dark room: forbidden.
  6. When non-Jew is paid: Mechaber lenient, Rama strict.
  7. Hinting: only indirect comments with no command and no mention of melacha are permitted.
  8. Amira l'amira (asking non-Jew to tell another non-Jew): lenient only in great loss; no positive benefit.
  9. Time-switches: permitted - all Poskim except Rav Moshe.
  10. Psik reisha: Rama is lenient for amira l'akum; Mishnah Berurah lenient for de'lo nichah leih in Shabbos need.

Hilchos Muktza

1. Foundations

Status of the Prohibition Muktza is an issur miderabanan. Rashi (Pesachim 47b) appeared to hold it has a Torah source, but Tosefos (Pesachim 30a) definitively ruled otherwise. The halacha follows Tosefos.

The halacha follows the Mishnah Berurah (=Rach): Rebbe Shimon Halacha always, except Nolad on Yom Tov.

Nolad - Something That Came into Existence on Shabbos Nolad is a sub-category of Muktza for items that did not exist (or were in a totally different state) before Shabbos. Examples:

  • An egg laid on Shabbos - Muktza according to all opinions.
  • Seeds that were in fruit at the onset of Shabbos and fell out on Shabbos - Muktza according to Rebbe Yehuda; Rebbe Shimon is lenient on Shabbos.
  • Condensation from an air conditioner - Nolad according to all opinions; assur to move.

2. The Five Categories of Muktza

Category 1: Muktza Machmas Gufa Items with no conceivable Shabbos use - they are inherently set aside.

  • Examples: Stones, raw money, dirt.
  • For food: requires (a) dechiya beyadayim - an active act that made it inedible, AND (b) lo chazi - it is totally inedible. E.g., figs placed on a roof to dry (grogeres, tzimukim).
  • For non-food items (e.g., stones): the two conditions above are not required.
  • Rambam: only condition for food is that it is totally inedible - no requirement of dechiya beyadayim. Mechaber rules like Rashi/Tosefos: both conditions required for food.
p. 207Muktza

Category 2: Muktza Machmas Chisaron Kis Items the person is careful to use only for their designated purpose, never to use them casually, and keeps in a set place - out of fear of financial loss.

  • Examples: Expensive tools, a mohel's scalpel, writing instruments (lechatchila - Rav Berkovits).
  • A wallet: The Elyah Rabbah rules it is generally Kli shemelachto l'issur, not Muktza machmas chisaron kis, since most people also keep non-Muktza items in their wallet.
  • A bed designated for money: If money is on it during Bein hashmashos, the bed becomes a bassis (see Section 4).
  • Wall clocks/paintings: Chazon Ish rules they are Muktza machmas chisaron kis. Rav Moshe Feinstein disagrees - they stay in place for function/decoration, not fear of damage.
  • Pesach cutlery: Rav Auerbach rules it is NOT Muktza machmas chisaron kis - the reason the person is careful with it is not one of the reasons recognized by Chazal.

Category 3: Muktza Machmas Issur Items that were forbidden to use as Shabbos began, where there was also dechiya beyadayim (an active act that caused the forbidden state).

  • Classic example: A candle lit before Shabbos. The act of lighting = dechiya beyadayim; it is forbidden to move while lit. After it goes out, Rebbe Meir holds it remains Muktza. Rebbe Shimon permits - he expected it to go out (yoshev u'metsapeh).
  • Halacha follows Rebbe Meir (strict) re: Muktza machmas issur. The Mechaber rules a lit candle's wax remains Muktza after it goes out.
  • Animal carcass that died on Shabbos: NOT Muktza machmas issur - there was no dechiya beyadayim; the person did nothing to cause the issur. Permitted to feed to one's animals (Mechaber, SA 324:7).
  • Vegetable cut by a non-Jew on Shabbos: IS Muktza machmas issur - the Beis Yosef explains that failing to pick it before Shabbos is itself treated as dechiya beyadayim (a minor act to do; not doing it shows intent).

Category 4: Kli Shemelachto L'issur A utensil whose primary function is forbidden on Shabbos, but which has possible permitted uses. This is a more lenient category of Muktza - it may be moved in two situations:

Permitted Reason to MoveExamples
L'sorech gufo - to use the kli itself for a permitted purposeUsing a hammer to crack open nuts; using a knife for food.

  • Kli shemelachto l'issur with NO possible permitted use at all: Baal HaMeor rules it is Muktza machmas gufa. Rashba rules it remains Kli shemelachto l'issur. Rav Moshe and Rav Berkovits rule leniently like the Rashba. Examples: umbrellas, thermometers, telephones.

Category 5: Kli Shemelachto L'heter A utensil whose primary function is permitted on Shabbos. It may be moved for any purpose, including l'tsorech gufo or even without any need at all. Examples: plates, cups, books.

1. Foundations - Definition of Memachek

Memachek ('smoothing') is one of the 39 Melachos. Its origin in the Mishkan was smoothing the hides used in construction.

ViewDistinction Between Memachek & Gozez
